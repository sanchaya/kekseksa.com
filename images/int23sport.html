The Grand National took place on 28 March 1924 with the King once more in attendance as the year before but without his towo sons. A relatively uneventful race was won by Master Robert ridden by Bob Trudgill. During post-race celebrations hosted by the owner, Lord Airlie, at Liverpool's Adelphi Hotel, the jockey suppsoedly re-enacted his victory by leaping a "Becher's Brook" made out of twenty magnums of Champagne.

As in the previous year, British Pathé made the most of its "exclusive", deploying a supposedly record number of forty-five cameramen and once again making liberal use of slow-moton photography.

Pathé also produced a much shorter version for the 9.5mm home-view Pathé-Baby system which was designed as a sort of companion-piece to the film of the 1922 Grand Steeplechase de Paris.

Ther may have been companion-films of the two events nw in the Pathé-Bbay and Pathéscope catalogues, but in fact the two events, until then broady equivalent, would part company quite sharply in 1924. A new design of the jumps and obstacles by Colonel Maurice Gillois, a former commandant at the Saumur military college was objected to even by some trainers in France but would prove distinctly off-putting for the English and Irish horse-owners who expected the jumps to be more "natural" in conception and would largely stop sending their horses to compete, making the Grand Steeplechase a more or less all-French affair.
